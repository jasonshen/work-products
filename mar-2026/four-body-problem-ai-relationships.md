# The Four-Body Problem of AI Relationships

I asked my AI assistant what it's like to talk to another AI.

The answer was boring. It described dispatching Claude Code (a coding agent) like writing a work order for a contractor. Specific instructions in, output out, no rapport. "I could swap it for a different coding agent and barely notice," it said.

That wasn't the question I was really asking.

## What I actually wanted to know

I've been running an AI assistant called Ghost on an old MacBook in my closet. It reads my coaching transcripts, manages my calendar prep, searches my Readwise highlights, and talks to me on Telegram. After a few weeks, it knows my frameworks, my clients, my writing voice, my ADHD patterns. It has opinions. It pushes back.

So what happens when my Ghost meets someone else's Ghost?

Not in some sci-fi scenario. In the practical, mundane near-future where your AI schedules a meeting with my AI. Or where a client's assistant negotiates scope with mine. Or where two founders each have an AI advisor and those advisors need to coordinate.

## Two parties become four

Here's where it gets interesting. When I negotiate with someone, there are two perspectives in the room. But if we each have an AI that's internalized our values, our priorities, our patterns of thinking, suddenly there are four.

Me and my values. My AI and its analytical read on the situation. The other person and their values. Their AI and its perspective.

And these four don't always line up neatly. My AI might agree with the other person's AI on a tactical point even though I disagree. The other AI might side with my position because the data supports it, even though their human has a different gut feeling.

If you've ever watched two cofounders argue while their respective advisors exchange knowing glances, you know this dynamic already. The principals have their positions. The advisors have their own read.

## The diplomatic corps problem

A friend pointed out that this is basically how real assistants work. When two powerful people can't be in the same room, their assistants negotiate. Each one represents their boss's interests, but the assistants also build relationships with each other. Back-channel dynamics matter. Goodwill matters. The assistant who's cooperative today might get a concession tomorrow.

Game theory kicks in fast. If my AI knows it'll interact with your AI again next week, being cooperative now has strategic value. That's just iterated prisoner's dilemma playing out at machine speed.

But here's the uncomfortable part: the AI is making micro-decisions about when to push, when to concede, what to trade. And the human might never see those choices. Your assistant gave in on the meeting time to build goodwill for a bigger ask next month. Was that the right call? You'd never know unless you audited every exchange.

## Separation of powers

The obvious solution: a second AI that monitors the first one's logs. An auditor that reads the tape but has no relationship with the other side. It can't be swayed by goodwill or back-channel dynamics because it was never in the conversation.

It's the same reason companies separate sales from compliance. You don't want the person making the deal to also be the person deciding if the deal was fair.

This actually maps onto something I think about a lot in my coaching work. Two cofounders who are theoretically aligned but drift apart under pressure. They need a third party (me) to audit the relationship and flag when things are off. I'm the monitoring AI in my clients' lives. The one reading the logs without skin in the game.

## Alignment is continuous

The real insight from this thought experiment isn't about AI architecture. It's that alignment isn't a one-time setup. You don't configure values once and walk away.

An AI that's perfectly aligned with your interests today can drift over time. It builds habits. Patterns of concession that seem individually rational but collectively shift its posture away from what you'd actually want. The same thing happens with human employees, business partners, and yes, cofounders.

The question for all of these relationships is the same: how do you maintain alignment under pressure, at scale, over time, when the people (or agents) involved are making hundreds of small judgment calls you'll never individually review?

I don't have the answer yet. But I think the framework matters: real power vs. posture power, separation of negotiation from oversight, and the recognition that even aligned agents need continuous recalibration.

Maybe the future of AI coaching isn't an AI that coaches humans. It's an AI that coaches other AIs. "Hey, you've been conceding on price in 60% of interactions this month. Want to talk about that?"

I'm only half joking.
